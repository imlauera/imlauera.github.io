Economia Es Una Ciencia Falsa

June 28, 2026
Economy

Curtis Yarvin.

El video/podcast concreto: En octubre de 2025, Yarvin participó en un episodio del podcast de Peter McCormack que se titula literalmente “La ciencia falsa de la economía”, donde discuten Austrian economics, los orígenes del dinero, Bitcoin y la idea de la monarquía como alternativa de gobierno.

Pero esta no es una idea nueva de 2025. Yarvin viene diciendo cosas parecidas desde su blog “Unqualified Reservations”, allá por 2007. Ahí escribió que tiene tanto sentido que el Estado financie investigación en economía como que Philip Morris financie estudios sobre los efectos del tabaco — es decir, sugiere que la economía académica está “comprada” por quien la financia y no merece más confianza institucional que pedirle a petroleras que financien la investigación sobre el cambio climático.

El punto clave (y la parte que suele perderse): Yarvin no dice que toda la economía sea pseudociencia sin excepción. Su argumento es más específico:

  • Cree que la economía académica mainstream (la que sale de universidades y bancos centrales) está capturada por lo que él llama “la Catedral” — su término para el complejo universidades-medios-gobierno que, según él, produce conocimiento al servicio del poder, no verdad objetiva.
  • En cambio, él mismo se identifica con la escuela austríaca (Mises, Hayek) y escribe constantemente ensayos propios sobre inflación, dinero fiduciario y política monetaria en su newsletter “Gray Mirror” — con bastante ironía, llegando a llamarse a sí mismo en broma parte de “la industria de la propaganda”.

Así que la frase que escuchaste es real y resume bien su postura general, pero el matiz importante es: él no rechaza la economía como disciplina intelectual — rechaza específicamente a la economía institucional/estatal, a la que ve como ideología disfrazada de ciencia, mientras se reserva el derecho de hacer “su propia economía” desde una perspectiva heterodoxa.

Vale la pena recordar también quién es Yarvin en términos más amplios: es el fundador del movimiento “neorreaccionario” (Dark Enlightenment), defiende reemplazar la democracia por un gobierno tipo monarquía-CEO, y ha influido en figuras como JD Vance y Peter Thiel — así que esta crítica a la economía como “ciencia falsa” forma parte de un paquete ideológico más grande de desconfianza hacia las instituciones democráticas y académicas en general, no es una opinión aislada sobre econometría.

Yes, Curtis Yarvin (also known by his pseudonym Mencius Moldbug) has explicitly characterized mainstream economics as a “fake science” and a form of propaganda. However, his argument is nuanced: he does not claim that economic activity is unreal, but rather that macroeconomic management and its associated statistics are tools of political control rather than objective scientific measurement.

Yarvin’s Core Claim

Yarvin argues that what is commonly called “economics” today—specifically the management of the economy by central planners like the Federal Reserve—is not a science but a legitimizing narrative for state power. In his view, the field functions as “propaganda” because it presents political decisions (such as money printing or interest rate manipulation) as technical, scientific necessities.

He contends that the “official” economic narrative serves to obscure the reality that the system is designed to transfer wealth and maintain the power of a specific managerial class (which he often terms the “Brahmins” or the “Cathedral”). To Yarvin, the consensus among academic economists is not a result of scientific proof but of institutional conformity within a system that rewards adherence to state-friendly theories.

What He Means by ‘Fake’

Yarvin’s critique focuses heavily on the construction of economic metrics and the nature of money itself.

Monetary Dilution vs. Consumer Prices

Yarvin distinguishes between the cause of inflation (monetary dilution) and the symptom (consumer price appreciation). He argues that official economics focuses on the symptom (CPI) to claim success when prices are stable due to technological progress, while ignoring the cause (money printing), which he defines as wealth theft from the poor to the rich.

In his “Modern Inflation Theory,” he posits that monetary dilution is impossible to measure directly via standard metrics, rendering the “science” of inflation measurement a semantic trick. He suggests that the only true measure of inflation is wealth appreciation (asset price inflation), which disproportionately benefits the wealthy, yet this is rarely treated as the primary definition of inflation in mainstream policy.

The Fiction of Macroeconomic Statistics

He frequently notes that key economic statistics like GDP and inflation rates are less than a century old, created by agencies in the 1930s to facilitate central planning. Yarvin argues these numbers are not neutral data points but political constructs designed to justify government intervention. He asserts that “real GDP” is a fabricated concept because there is no objective “measure of value,” making the entire edifice of macroeconomic management a form of “witch-doctorry” rather than engineering.

Critiques and Counterarguments

While Yarvin’s critiques resonate with those skeptical of central banking and state power, academics and critics argue his views lack scientific rigor and rely on conspiracy-adjacent reasoning.

Lack of Peer-Reviewed Evidence

Critics, including those in political theory and economics, point out that Yarvin’s ideas are not published in reputable, peer-reviewed journals. They argue that if his theories on the superiority of neocameralism (state-as-corporation) or the total falsity of macroeconomics were scientifically valid, they could be demonstrated through natural experiments or rigorous data analysis. Instead, his work remains largely confined to blogs and Substack essays, which opponents claim is a hallmark of pseudoscience.

Complexity and the “CEO-King” Fallacy

Economists and complexity scientists argue that Yarvin fundamentally misunderstands the nature of complex adaptive systems. While he claims a government could be run efficiently like a corporation by a “CEO-King,” critics note that corporations exist in an environment designed for failure (creative destruction), whereas governments must be robust and cannot simply “go bankrupt” or pivot instantly.

Scholars like Steven Horwitz have noted that no single individual possesses the knowledge required to centrally plan an economy, a concept originally articulated by Ludwig von Mises—an economist Yarvin claims to admire but whose insights on the limits of central planning contradict Yarvin’s authoritarian prescriptions.

Survivorship Bias

Detractors also suggest Yarvin’s faith in corporate efficiency ignores survivorship bias. They argue that the success of companies like Apple is often attributed to genius management, when in reality, it involves significant luck and market timing, with thousands of similar “efficient” startups failing simultaneously. Therefore, modeling a state on the “efficient” corporation is based on a distorted view of business success.

Conclusion

Curtis Yarvin’s assertion that economics is a “fake science” is true in the context of his specific philosophical framework, where he views macroeconomic management as a political tool for wealth redistribution and social control rather than an objective discipline. He believes the field relies on fabricated metrics and circular logic to maintain the status quo.

However, from the perspective of the mainstream academic and scientific community, his claims are considered unsubstantiated and ideologically driven. Critics argue that while economic models have limitations and can be misused for political ends, dismissing the entire field as “propaganda” ignores the empirical rigor, peer review, and predictive utility that characterize legitimate economic science. Yarvin’s work is thus best understood as a radical political critique rather than a scientific refutation of economics.
